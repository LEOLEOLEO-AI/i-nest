## Core Contribution
Demonstrates that self-organized criticality (SOC) in cortical networks arises from specific complex network topologies, particularly scale-free architectures with varying degrees of small-worldness, influenced by connectivity rules and the balance of excitation/inhibition.

## Next Steps for iNEST
Validate findings in vivo with advanced imaging/recording techniques., Explore dynamical causal modeling to test topology-criticality causality., Investigate how learning algorithms can induce similar topological features in artificial networks., Study the impact of neuromodulators on topology and criticality.

## Key Findings
- Critical states emerge in specific complex network topologies, not randomly.
- Scale-free architectures with varying small-worldness best explain experimental variability in spontaneous dynamics.
- Connectivity rules (e.g., balance between excitation/inhibition, percentage of inhibitory hubs) drive networks toward different dynamic states.
- Simulated cortical networks achieve a critical regime when excitation/inhibition balance and inhibitory hub presence are appropriately tuned.

## Research Gaps
- How specific connectivity rules evolve or are learned in biological networks.
- Causal evidence linking topological features directly to functional criticality in vivo.
- Role of critical states in cognitive functions like memory or decision-making.
- Generalizability to non-cortical or artificial network architectures.
