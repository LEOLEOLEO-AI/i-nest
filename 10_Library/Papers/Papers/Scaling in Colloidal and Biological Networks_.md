# Scaling in Colloidal and Biological Networks.

- **Year**: 2020
- **Source**: pubmed
- **URL**: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33286394/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33286394/)
- **Authors**: Nosonovsky M, Roy P

## iNEST Relevance
**Score**: 0.80 | **Themes**: Physical Substrate, Information & Computation, Emergence & Self-Organization
The paper directly studies how complex network intelligence (in biological systems) emerges from physical substrates (colloidal networks) and self-organizing processes. It bridges physics, information theory (Shannon entropy), and neuroscience, aligning with iNEST's core mission to understand intelligence as a physical, emergent phenomenon. It provides foundational principles (scaling, topology) relevant for engineering neuromorphic systems.

## Core Contribution
Applies scaling, dimensional analysis, and information theory (Shannon entropy) to identify universal structural and dynamic principles across diverse physical and biological networks, demonstrating how fractal, scale-free, and small-world properties emerge from fundamental physical interactions and self-organization.

## Next Steps for iNEST
Develop integrated models that combine the identified scaling principles with specific learning algorithms or adaptive dynamics. Extend the analysis to temporal networks and non-equilibrium dynamics to better capture living systems. Explore how to artificially induce self-organized criticality in engineered networks for robust computation.

## Key Findings
- Networks in colloidal science exhibit self-organizing properties driven by percolation and self-organized criticality.
- Allometric scaling laws are identified in branching vascular networks.
- In hierarchical cortical neural networks, the characteristic time constant is independent of brain size in interspecies comparisons.
- Shannon entropy effectively quantifies the information content within these diverse network structures.
- Fractal, scale-free, and small-world properties are common across the studied physical and biological systems.

## Research Gaps
- No unified model explaining how intelligence-specific functions (learning, memory, adaptation) emerge from the identified scaling and topological properties.
- Gap between descriptive scaling laws and predictive, testable theories of network-based intelligence emergence.
- Limited exploration of how the *dynamics* on these networks (not just structure) give rise to complex computation.
- The role of specific physical interactions (beyond pairwise) in shaping network intelligence is not deeply explored.
