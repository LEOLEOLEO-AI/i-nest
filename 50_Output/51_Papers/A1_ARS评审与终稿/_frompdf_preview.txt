L56: [52] The sustainability crisis of artificial intelligence
L57: [89] The trajectory of modern AI development is defined by a single operating principle: scale
L58: [89] compute, and intelligence will follow. Each generation of frontier LLMs has required sub-
L59: [94] stantially greater training compute than its predecessor, with scaling law analyses projecting
L60: [90] continued exponential growth [1]. Inference energy has grown proportionally. Yet empirical
L61: [89] scaling laws now reveal that capability improvements per unit energy expenditure follow a
L62: [90] sub-linear curve—each successive generation buys less intelligence per joule invested. The
L63: [84] global AI industry is approaching a thermodynamic asymptote—one enforced not by CMOS
L64: [93] fabrication technology per se, but by the binary digital logic paradigm: the current paradigm
L65: [89] can produce ever more capable functional systems, but the energy cost required to sustain
L66: [87] them grows without bound while the gap between these systems and genuine general intel-
L67: [23] ligence does not close.
L68: [82] This is not merely a resource problem. It is a symptom of pursuing the wrong quan-
L69: [83] tity. The dominant paradigm equates intelligence with compute—more parameters, more
L70: [81] data, more hardware—and measures progress by benchmark performance. But benchmark
L71: [87] performance and intelligence emergence are orthogonal dimensions. GPT-class models sur-
L72: [83] pass most humans on standardized tests in law, medicine, and coding. Yet as we show
L73: [84] below, GPT-2—a representative large-scale open-weight language model—scores approxi-
L74: [90] mately 30-fold lower than the human brain on the metric of emergent intelligence potential
L75: [88] (CST = 0 .056 vs. 3 .909), and even below Caenorhabditis elegans , a 279-neuron nematode
L76: [91] (CST = 0 .357 under correct graded-potential physics). This is not a contradiction. It is a
L77: [51] revelation: we have been measuring the wrong thing.
