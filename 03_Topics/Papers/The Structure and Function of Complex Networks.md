## Core Contribution
This foundational review paper synthesizes and organizes the emerging field of complex network science, establishing key concepts, models, and analytical techniques for understanding the structure and dynamics of real-world networked systems across physical, social, and biological domains.

## Key Findings
- Real-world networks (Internet, social, biological) are not random but possess distinct structural properties like scale-free degree distributions and high clustering.
- The 'small-world' property (short average path lengths) is common across diverse networks, enabling efficient information/propagule flow.
- Preferential attachment is a key generative mechanism for scale-free networks, explaining power-law degree distributions.
- Network topology fundamentally influences dynamical processes like epidemic spreading, synchronization, and resilience.
- Random graph models provide null benchmarks, but new models (WS, BA) are needed to capture real-world clustering and degree distributions.

## Research Gaps
- Intelligence as an emergent network property is not explicitly addressed; the focus is on generic dynamics (spreading, failure).
- Design and optimization of networks for specific intelligent functions (learning, adaptation).
- Integration of network structure with node-level computational or cognitive capabilities.
- Real-time, adaptive rewiring in physical networks as a form of collective intelligence.
