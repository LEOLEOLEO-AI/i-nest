1. 引言：八十年范式的结构性危机
"Computing's energy problem: the key to scaling computing performance is to create applications and hardware which are better matched to the task and each other." —— Mark Horowitz, ISSCC 2014 Plenary [1]

1945年，von Neumann在《EDVAC报告初稿》中描绘了存储程序计算机：处理单元、控制单元、存储器与输入输出经总线相连。这一蓝图的核心哲学是将计算锚定于"节点"——处理器为主角，存储器为配角，互连总线为仆从。IBM Research的Le Gallo-Bourdeau精确概括了其统治力："冯·诺依曼架构非常灵活，这是它最大的优点，这就是它最初被采用、也是它至今仍是主流架构的原因。"[2]

八十年后，这一范式正面临结构性危机。危机并非源自单一技术突破，而是三堵墙的交叉共振。

存储墙（memory wall）。 Wulf与McKee于1995年首次命名了处理器与存储器之间每年约50%的性能差距 [4]。其后三十年间，乱序执行、推测执行、多级缓存与硬件预取不断延缓冲击，但物理根源始终未被触及。Dennard缩放约于2006年失效后，逻辑运算能耗随电压降低持续改善，而DRAM依赖电容充放电——其物理机制不受逻辑工艺缩放惠及，遂出现"剪刀差"。

通信墙（communication wall）。 当系统从单芯片多核扩展至万卡集群，通信开销的增长速度超过计算能力。在典型高性能计算（HPC）部署中，互连网络可占系统满载功率的相当比例，通信占消息传递接口（MPI）程序执行时间的若干个百分点至两成以上 [5,6]。在大模型分布式训练中，AllReduce等集合通信可占据单步训练时间的可观份额。

能量墙（energy wall）。 Horowitz在45 nm工艺下给出标尺：32位浮点乘法约3.7 pJ，而片外DRAM访问高达约1.3–2.6 nJ，相差数百倍 [1]。MIT Eyeriss团队在65 nm下确认：片外DRAM读取约为一次乘累加（MAC）的200倍 [7]。IBM Research在2025年给出决定性判断：在AI工作负载中计算约占10%，数据搬运约占90% [2]。

这三堵墙发生于不同层级——处理器–存储器接口、节点间网络、全系统能量流——但指向同一物理本质：在冯·诺依曼范式中，是数据搬运而非数据加工决定了能效、性能与可扩展性。本文将这三堵墙统一命名为"数据移动墙"（Data Movement Wall）。 置于可持续智能计算的框架下，形势更显紧迫：当前AI训练能耗的增速远超Moore定律的补偿能力，若"数据移动占90%"这一比例不变，增加可再生能源供给只能延缓而无法根除矛盾。真正的可持续性必须来自对数据移动本身的架构级优化。

2. 第一性原理：计算的算子–数据移动分解
2.1 计算的形式化视角
任何计算任务 T 可表示为有向无环图 G = (V, E)，其中节点 V 代表基本运算（算子），边 E 代表数据依赖（数据移动）。计算的执行即：在正确的时刻，将正确的数据，经正确的路径，送达正确的算子。该DAG模型是并行计算理论的标准工具 [25,26]。

2.2 弱耦合分解
由此得到计算的弱耦合分解：

T=O(V)∘M(E)+Γ(O,M)
其中 O(V) 为算子空间，M(E) 为数据移动空间，Γ(O, M) 为耦合项。此处采用"弱耦合"而非"正交"，因算子选择与数据移动模式之间确有耦合（如Winograd卷积同时改变乘法次数与数据流），但这种耦合属高阶效应：算子语义（"做什么"）与数据移动语义（"怎么送"）可独立描述、编程与优化。

口径定义。 本文显式区分两种数据移动口径。广义数据移动覆盖从寄存器堆至跨节点网络的所有层级（Horowitz [1] 与Eyeriss [7] 的能耗数据属此口径）；狭义互连路由仅指计算单元/芯片/节点间的互连传输（HPC通信数据 [5,6] 与SDI机制属此口径）。两者为包含关系：Routing_narrow ⊂ Movement_broad。

2.3 数据移动主导定律
设总能耗 E_total = E_op + E_move，定义数据移动能量比 η = E_move / E_total。基于Horowitz（45 nm）[1] 与Eyeriss（65 nm）[7] 的实测数据，Table 1给出能耗标尺。

Table 1 算子与各层级数据移动的能耗对比

操作 / 层级	能耗（45 nm）	相对MAC倍数（65 nm）	口径
8位整数加法	0.03 pJ	—	算子
32位浮点乘法	3.7 pJ	—	算子
寄存器堆（0.5 kB）	—	1×	广义移动

由此归纳出数据移动主导定律：在冯·诺依曼范式下，对于数据规模超出片上可复用容量的计算任务，数据移动能耗始终为主导项——AI场景中 η ≥ 0.9，HPC与信号处理中 η ≈ 0.5–0.8，所有数据密集型场景中 η > 0.5。

该定律可由Roofline模型 [9] 获得理论支撑：当应用算术强度低于硬件"拐点"时，性能受限于带宽而非计算。层次化Roofline模型 [9b] 进一步揭示，Stencil计算（算术强度 OI ≈ 0.33–0.56 FLOP/Byte [10]）与深度学习推理深陷多级"带宽受限"区域。这意味着：在可持续智能计算的议程中，优化数据移动的杠杆效应是优化算子的数倍。

3. 四大计算场景的数据移动解剖
第2节建立了"数据移动是核心矛盾"的总体判断，本节将其置于四个具体场景中解剖验证。

3.1 通用计算
通用CPU的多级缓存体系（L1–L2–L3–DRAM）本质上是一个层次化数据移动网络，每一次Cache Miss都是一次数据移动失败。Horowitz的实测数据 [1] 给出精确标尺：一条指令的控制开销（取指、时钟、流水线寄存器）约70 pJ，而ALU执行的加法/乘法仅0.1–3.7 pJ。取指、访存、缓存一致性协议（MESI/MOESI）、TLB翻译全部属于广义数据移动。即使在看似"计算密集"的通用计算中，相当大比例的能耗也消耗在数据移动上。

3.2 智能计算
AI推理与训练的核心是矩阵乘法（GEMM），其算子极简——乘累加（MAC）。差异化不在算子，而在数据流。MIT Eyeriss定义了权重驻留、输出驻留、无本地复用与行驻留等数据流，每种本质上是一种数据移动策略；其行驻留（row-stationary）之所以能效最优，在于其在寄存器堆、PE阵列与全局缓冲三个层级同时最大化了数据复用 [7]。在系统层面，Cerebras WSE-3以单晶圆集成4万亿晶体管、90万个AI核心与44 GB片上SRAM，在同等功耗下相对上一代实现性能翻倍 [12]——其核心优势不是算子更快，而是片上fabric彻底消除了芯片间数据移动。IBM NorthPole在30亿参数LLM推理中，相对最节能GPU快47倍、相对最低延迟GPU能效高73倍 [35]，再次印证：在AI计算中，互连即性能。

3.3 高性能计算
超算的分布式本质使通信拓扑成为性能骨架。Stencil计算的算术强度仅0.33–0.56 FLOP/Byte [10]——每做一次浮点运算需搬运2–3字节；HPC通信占执行时间的若干百分点至两成以上 [5,6]。Frontier、Fugaku等顶级超算的设计演进反复验证：互连拓扑（Dragonfly、Fat-tree、Torus）的选择对实际性能的影响往往超过计算节点的浮点峰值。在最需要性能的领域，网络比节点更重要。

3.4 信号处理
快速傅里叶变换（FFT）是信号处理基石——N 点FFT分解为 log₂N 级蝶形运算。FFT的真正精妙不在蝶形算子（始终是同一复数乘加），而在级间数据重排的移动模式；SDF、MDF、MDC等FFT硬件架构的本质差异，正是不同数据移动拓扑的实现方式。H. T. Kung于1978年提出的脉动阵列 [27] 是数据移动优化的历史先驱："脉动架构通过在每次内存访问中执行多次计算，可在不增加I/O带宽需求的情况下加速计算密集型问题。"[28]

3.5 跨场景统合
Table 2 四大场景的算子与数据移动特征对比

6. 软件定义互连：从固化到可编程
冯·诺依曼架构的根本缺陷在于：互连路由在设计时固化，而非运行时可变。CPU缓存层级、GPU脉动阵列数据流、ASIC的片上网络（NoC）均在流片时确定，工作负载变化时无法自适应。NVIDIA从NVLink到NVSwitch再到NVLink-C2C的演进，本质上是在不断打补丁以缓解这一僵化 [20]；MAERI（ASPLOS 2018）[21] 提出"使加速器内部互连可重构"，正是SDI思想在片上的萌芽。

6.1 SDI三层架构
SDI将互连路由从硬件固化层提升至软件可编程层，使拓扑在运行时可动态重构。其物理层为可编程交叉互连矩阵，支持任意端口对间连接的建立与断开（MIT Lincoln Lab的active wafer-scale fabric [22] 与Lightmatter Passage光子互连 [22b] 展示了物理可实现性）；路由层基于元语的软件编译，将每个元语映射为物理层的一组开关状态；编排层依据计算DAG与硬件状态，动态决定算子–路由的联合优化。核心方程为

7. 范式迁移：液态一体架构
算子收敛性回答了"为何优化算子不够"，元语标准化回答了"如何系统化管理数据移动"，SDI回答了"用什么机制实现可编程互连"。本节将三者整合为统一架构。

7.1 两种范式的对决
Table 5 节点中心范式与网络中心范式的对比

液态架构的适用条件严格限于"密集计算负载"，它不替代通用CPU。IBM的Burr的判断是准确的："未来很可能是冯·诺依曼与非冯·诺依曼处理器的混合体——各自处理它们最擅长的运算。"[2]

7.2 六条路径的统一
液态架构统一了六条既有非冯路径：存内计算（IMC）将存储–计算距离缩至零，液态架构可将IMC核心作为PE节点而SDI提供核心间互连；近存计算（NMC）以IBM NorthPole为代表（LLM推理能效高73倍 [35]），液态PE可采近存设计加SDI互连；数据流架构（如SambaNova RDU [33]）的数据驱动执行被液态架构从编译时固定提升到运行时可编程；CGRA通常限于单芯片，液态架构经晶圆级集成扩展至介观尺度；更强互连（NVLink/UCIe/光互连 [20,22b]）提供物理层带宽，SDI在逻辑层提供可编程性，两者正交组合；算法优化（融合/压缩/稀疏化）决定"搬多少"，SDI决定"怎么搬"，二者互补。这六条路径在液态架构中首次被统一于单一框架，而非各自为战。

7.4 物理实现、良率与编程模型
液态硬件是介观尺度上以互连为第一公民的硬件平台，其关键路径包括晶圆级集成（Cerebras WSE-3：90万核心、44 GB片上SRAM [12]）、Chiplet加UCIe/NVLink-C2C [20]、光电混合互连 [22b] 与可重构数据流单元 [33]。晶圆级集成的良率挑战已被Cerebras缓解——通过冗余核心与缺陷绕过实现高硅利用率 [12]；SDI机制天然容错，可在运行时动态绕过故障PE或链路。其编程模型为"计算图编译 + 算子–数据移动联合调度"：高层计算图（PyTorch/DAG）→ 编译器分解为标准算子加元语组合 → 基于代价模型联合优化 → 运行时SDI动态配置。Cerebras软件框架（原生支持PyTorch [12]）与SambaNova SambaFlow [33] 已提供工业原型验证。

9. 展望（Outlook）：从理论到实证
将上述理论框架转化为可实证检验的研究议程，本文提出五项。其一，数据移动能效基准：在固定互连与SDI上对比测量 Bits-moved/Joule，并按广义/狭义口径分解端到端能耗。其二，SDI收益阈值实证：在CGRA或可编程NoC平台上实测 ΔE_move 与 E_cfg，验证收益阈值不等式，并扫描重构频率对吞吐的影响。其三，可扩展性曲线：测量端到端效率随PE数的扩展关系，验证SDI在"拓扑多变"条件下是否较固定拓扑具备更优的强/弱扩展特性。其四，元语IR编译质量：在真实AI/HPC模型上评估元语中间表示加编译器的通信量压缩比、拥塞率与数据复用率。其五，涌现阈值初步实验：系统化改变网络拓扑参数（度数、连通性、小世界系数），测量适应性任务表现与拓扑复杂度的关系，寻找相变阈值。

10. 结论
"We're not running out of compute—we're running out of the ability to move data to the compute."

本文的五项结论如下。其一，数据移动墙是根本瓶颈。 Horowitz、Eyeriss、IBM Research、Dally与Arteris的多源数据共同确认：所有数据密集型场景中数据移动能耗均为主导项（η > 0.5，AI中 η ≥ 0.9），且随Dennard缩放终结与DRAM停滞，能耗剪刀差在先进工艺中持续扩大。其二，算子收敛性关闭了旧路径。 四大场景的原子算子收敛于不超过十个的有限集合，高阶数学均可归约，优化算子已接近收益递减边界。其三，数据移动可标准化与可编程。 十一个元语实现标准化并附带代价模型，SDI在收益阈值条件满足时具有明确优势，SambaNova RDU与AMD Versal ACAP已提供工业验证。其四，液态架构是统一框架。 液态架构 = 标准算子库 ⊕ 标准元语 ⊕ SDI矩阵，首次将IMC、NMC、数据流、CGRA、更强互连与算法优化六条路径统一于单一框架。其五，可持续智能计算的物理路径。 若AI中90%能耗在数据搬运，则仅靠可再生能源或算子优化无法根除危机；液态架构经SDI从物理层消除冗余搬运，直接攻击那90%。计算的下一个八十年，不再属于更快的处理器，而属于更智能的网络。

一种支持网络中心计算的软件定义互连系统架构及调度方法
说明书摘要
本发明公开了一种支持网络中心计算的软件定义互连系统架构及调度方法。该方法将计算任务的算子与数据移动解耦,把互连路由从设计时固化提升为运行时可编程:接收以原语序列表示的计算指令流,经拓扑融合优化后,由软件定义互连控制器在运行时动态重构可编程互连矩阵的拓扑状态,使数据在重构后的拓扑中流动即完成对应的数学变换。该架构包含可编程互连矩阵、计算节点阵列、原语调度器和全局同步单元;其中拓扑配置寄存器采用当前寄存器与影子寄存器的双缓冲结构,在当前原语执行计算期间预加载下一原语所需拓扑至影子寄存器,并在计算完成时原子交换,使拓扑切换延迟被计算延迟完全隐藏,切换粒度达纳秒级。所述拓扑融合优化包括相邻拓扑合并、流水线预取与原语融合消除中间数据。本发明适用于大语言模型推理与训练、集合通信卸载、高性能科学计算与信号处理,可显著降低数据搬运开销。

说明书
技术领域
[0001] 本发明涉及集成电路体系结构与可重构计算技术领域,特别涉及一种面向智能计算与高性能计算、支持以互连网络为中心的计算范式的软件定义互连系统架构及其调度方法。

背景技术
[0002] 在现代智能计算与高性能计算中,数据搬运而非算术运算已成为系统能效与性能的决定性因素。在以处理器为中心的传统体系结构中,计算与存储分离,数据需在计算单元与存储单元之间反复往返;在多芯片、多节点的大规模并行场景中,集合通信开销随系统规模的增长速度超过计算能力的增长速度。在大语言模型的分布式推理与训练中,以全归约(AllReduce)、全交换(AlltoAll)为代表的集合通信操作可占据单步执行时间的相当比例,成为制约系统扩展性的主要瓶颈。

[0003] 现有缓解上述瓶颈的技术路线存在以下缺陷:

[0004] 第一,固定拓扑的互连体系无法适配多变的工作负载。当前主流图形处理器与张量处理器集群采用在流片时即确定的固定互连拓扑,如全连接、二维环面、胖树等。集合通信操作需在固定拓扑之上运行软件路由算法,形成"先在固定拓扑上规划路径、再搬运数据"的串行范式,导致路由延迟与计算延迟串行叠加,且固定拓扑难以同时对不同通信模式(归约、广播、全交换)均达到最优,互连资源利用率低。

[0005] 第二,通信与计算分属不同软件栈层级,无法在编译期或硬件层面融合。在现有体系中,集合通信操作在语义上被视为独立于计算的"通信开销",通信库与计算库分属不同抽象层级,例如由专用集合通信库负责通信、由基础线性代数库负责计算,二者的执行边界在硬件层面割裂,中间数据需写回存储后再重新读取,产生大量冗余数据搬运。

[0006] 第三,网内归约等在网计算方案受限于固定的归约结构。现有在网计算方案在交换节点中实现固定的树状归约结构,虽能减少穿越网络的数据量,但其归约拓扑在部署时即确定,无法在运行时按工作负载在归约、广播、全交换、蝶形等多种模式之间切换,亦不支持将路由与数学变换在语义层面统一。

[0007] 第四,光交换等可重构互连方案的重构延迟过大。现有基于光路交换的可重构拓扑方案虽可改变网络拓扑,但其拓扑重构延迟通常为毫秒量级,远大于计算操作的时间尺度,无法实现拓扑切换与细粒度计算操作的流水线重叠,因而难以应用于以单个算子为粒度的动态重构。

[0008] 综上,现有技术缺乏一种能够将互连拓扑在运行时以接近计算操作的时间粒度动态重构、并使拓扑切换开销被计算完全隐藏、同时将路由与数学变换在语义层面统一的软件定义互连架构与调度方法。

发明内容
[0009] 针对上述现有技术的缺陷,本发明提出一种支持网络中心计算的软件定义互连系统架构及调度方法。其核心构思在于:将互连路由从设计时固化提升为运行时可编程,把计算任务表达为一组覆盖通信、计算、数据与控制的原语序列,经拓扑融合优化后由软件定义互连控制器在运行时动态重构可编程互连矩阵,使数据在重构后的拓扑中流动的过程同时完成数据搬运与数学变换;并通过当前寄存器与影子寄存器构成的双缓冲拓扑配置结构,使拓扑切换延迟被计算延迟完全隐藏。

[0010] 本发明的技术效果包括:其一,通过运行时可编程的拓扑重构,消除"先规划路径、再搬运数据"的串行叠加,使路由与变换合一;其二,通过双缓冲影子寄存器的原子交换,将拓扑切换延迟降至纳秒级并被计算重叠隐藏,克服现有光交换方案毫秒级重构延迟的缺陷;其三,通过原语融合消除中间数据的写回与重读,降低冗余数据搬运;其四,通过统一的原语抽象层,兼容现有集合通信库与计算库,无需大规模改写上层应用。

[0011] 本发明的技术方案如下。

[0012] 一种支持网络中心计算的软件定义互连调度方法,包括以下步骤:

[0013] 步骤S1,接收计算任务的原语序列指令流。所述原语序列由对目标计算任务的计算图进行分解得到,所述原语属于一组覆盖通信、计算、数据与控制四类操作的原语集合,其中每个原语对应一次拓扑重构操作和/或一次本地计算操作。

[0014] 步骤S2,对所述原语序列执行拓扑融合优化,得到优化后的原语序列与对应的拓扑配置序列。所述拓扑融合优化包括相邻拓扑合并、流水线拓扑预取与原语融合消除中间数据中的至少一种。

[0015] 步骤S3,由软件定义互连控制器按所述拓扑配置序列,在运行时将可编程互连矩阵的拓扑状态从当前拓扑动态重构为下一拓扑;其中所述可编程互连矩阵由N×N个可独立配置通断状态的交叉开关构成,N为计算节点数。

[0016] 步骤S4,使分布在所述计算节点上的数据在重构后的拓扑中流动,流动过程同时完成数据从源节点到目标节点的搬运与对应的数学变换;即对于给定数学变换T与输入数据向量x,通过将互连拓扑重构为与T对应的拓扑状态,数据x流过该拓扑后的输出等于T(x)。

[0017] 步骤S5,由全局同步单元生成全局同步信号,使所有参与计算的节点在指定时钟周期数后达到同步屏障,以协调异步计算域之间的数据一致性。

[0018] 进一步地,所述步骤S3中的运行时拓扑重构采用双缓冲方式实现,具体为:所述可编程互连矩阵的拓扑状态由拓扑配置寄存器控制,所述拓扑配置寄存器包括当前寄存器与影子寄存器;在当前原语执行本地计算操作期间,由所述软件定义互连控制器将下一原语所需的拓扑配置预先写入所述影子寄存器;当当前原语的本地计算操作完成时,将所述影子寄存器的内容原子性地交换至所述当前寄存器,使下一原语的拓扑在其计算开始前已就绪,从而使拓扑切换延迟与本地计算延迟在时间上完全重叠。

[0020] (a)将互连拓扑重构为log₂N级蝶形网络,数据流过该蝶形网络后,每个节点的输出值等于输入向量的离散傅里叶变换的对应分量;

[0022] (c)将互连拓扑重构为多级交叉开关网络,使每个节点在单次重构周期内向其余各节点各发送一个数据块,等价于一次全交换操作;

[0024] (e)将互连拓扑重构为并行前缀网络,数据经上升与下降阶段后,每个节点获得输入向量前缀运算的对应分量。

具体实施方式
[0036] 以下结合具体实施例对本发明的技术方案进行详细说明。

[0037] 实施例一:大语言模型注意力层推理的原语调度与拓扑融合。 以单层多头注意力推理为例。输入激活张量经查询、键、值投影后计算注意力分数并归一化。原语调度器将该计算图分解为如下原语序列:先执行一次拓扑切换原语将互连矩阵重构为脉动阵列,随后连续执行三次矩阵乘法原语完成查询、键、值投影;此三次矩阵乘法原语共享同一脉动阵列拓扑,经步骤S2的相邻拓扑合并,三者之间无需额外拓扑切换。继而执行矩阵乘法原语计算注意力分数,执行逐元素映射原语完成缩放,执行沿轴归约原语取行最大值,执行逐元素映射原语完成指数运算,执行沿轴归约原语完成行求和,执行逐元素映射原语完成归一化。

[0038] 在上述序列中,取行最大值的归约原语执行计算期间,拓扑融合优化引擎已将后续逐元素映射所需拓扑预取至影子寄存器;当归约原语计算完成时,影子寄存器与当前寄存器原子交换,使逐元素映射原语的拓扑在其计算开始前已就绪。由此,本应串行的拓扑切换延迟被前一原语的计算延迟完全隐藏。

[0039] 实施例二:专家路由中的全交换分发与收集。 在含专家路由的前馈网络中,门控网络输出路由分数后选取若干专家。原语调度器以全交换原语实现令牌向专家节点的分发,经专家本地计算后再以全交换原语实现结果收集。区别于现有方案将全交换视为独立通信开销,本实施例中全交换原语经步骤S4将互连矩阵重构为多级交叉开关网络,数据流过该网络的过程既完成路由又完成数据重排,不再产生独立于计算的通信延迟。

[0040] 实施例三:N点快速傅里叶变换的运行时蝶形拓扑切换。 对N点快速傅里叶变换,原语调度器生成log₂N次循环,每次循环包含一次拓扑切换原语与一次逐元素映射原语。在第k级循环中,拓扑切换原语将互连矩阵重构为该级蝶形网络配置——即在节点i与节点i⊕2^(k-1)之间建立双向连接,其中⊕为按位异或;逐元素映射原语在各节点本地完成该级旋转因子的复数乘加。经流水线预取,第k+1级的拓扑切换与第k级的本地映射计算重叠。由于同一物理互连矩阵在不同循环中承载不同级的蝶形拓扑,本实施例区别于固化单一蝶形结构的专用硬件。

[0041] 实施例四:现有通信与计算库的兼容性映射。 兼容性映射单元将现有库的接口调用自动翻译为原语序列:全归约接口调用翻译为归约-广播原语;全交换接口调用翻译为全交换原语;广播接口调用翻译为广播原语;归约分散接口调用翻译为先归约原语后点对点搬运原语;全收集接口调用翻译为汇聚原语结合广播原语;矩阵乘法接口调用翻译为矩阵乘法原语,若伴随缩放系数或累加项则在矩阵乘法原语前后插入逐元素映射原语。由此,既有上层应用无需大规模改写即可获得拓扑融合带来的加速。

For eight decades, computing machines have been organized around the processor: the unit that calculates is treated as the protagonist, and the wires that carry data between units are treated as passive plumbing. This arrangement is now failing. In the workloads that define modern artificial intelligence, almost all of the energy is spent moving data rather than computing on it, and the fraction wasted on movement has grown for thirty years as the cost of arithmetic fell while the cost of fetching a number from memory did not. The deeper problem is structural: a machine whose connections are fixed when it is manufactured cannot match the constantly changing flow of data through a modern model. Here we show that treating the interconnect itself as the primary, programmable resource — a paradigm we call topology-centric computing — resolves this mismatch, and we derive the precise conditions under which making connections reconfigurable at runtime is guaranteed to pay for itself. We further show that a system's capacity for intelligent behaviour is bounded not by how fast it computes but by how richly its physical network can be reorganized. These results reframe the search for sustainable, capable computing as a search for smarter networks rather than faster processors.

Main text
Introduction
The von Neumann architecture has anchored computation on processor-centric nodes since 1945, an arrangement so successful that it remains the dominant template six decades later¹. Yet the assumption beneath it — that the interconnect is a transparent transport layer — has quietly become the binding constraint. Across modern artificial-intelligence (AI) workloads, the energy devoted to data movement now dwarfs that devoted to arithmetic²,³. A single access to off-chip memory costs hundreds of times the energy of a floating-point multiply², and this ratio has widened for three decades as transistor scaling improved logic efficiency while memory-access energy stagnated after the end of Dennard scaling⁴. The consequence is a thermodynamically unsustainable trajectory in which each generation of models consumes exponentially more energy for sub-linear gains⁵.

The prevailing response has been to strengthen the node: faster arithmetic units, taller memory stacks, more elaborate packaging. This logic is not wrong, but it is increasingly self-defeating. The more aggressively a node is strengthened, the more the system's performance is dictated by the relations among nodes — by which unit is connected to which, along which path, under which schedule⁶,⁷. Performance has become a property of the network, not of the device. We argue that the decisive conceptual unit must therefore shift from the node to the topology: the programmable structure of adjacency, path, locality and transformation that increasingly co-determines speed, energy and flexibility.

We name this paradigm topology-centric computing. Its central claim is that the interconnect should be promoted from passive substrate to a first-class computational resource, optimized jointly with arithmetic and storage. Realizing it requires a concrete mechanism — software-defined interconnect (SDI) — that elevates routing from design-time fixation to runtime programmability. Here we (i) formalize the operator–movement decomposition that motivates the paradigm; (ii) derive a closed-form benefit-threshold for SDI, establishing exactly when runtime reconfiguration is provably worthwhile; and (iii) establish an upper bound — the α-barrier — that links a network's reconfigurable complexity to its capacity for emergent intelligent behaviour, showing why no amount of arithmetic scaling within a fixed-topology, binary-digital substrate can cross it.

The operator–movement decomposition
Any computation can be represented as a directed acyclic graph in which vertices are operators and edges are data dependencies⁸. Executing it means delivering the right data, along the right path, to the right operator at the right time. This admits a weakly coupled decomposition into an operator space and a data-movement space (Fig. 1a). The two are independently describable: operator semantics specify what is computed; movement semantics specify how data are routed.

Two empirical regularities make this decomposition consequential. First, the operators that hardware executes converge to a small finite set. All higher-order mathematics — trigonometric, exponential and transcendental functions — reduces to no more than ten atomic primitives through polynomial approximation⁹ and shift-add methods¹⁰, so further operator optimization yields diminishing returns. Second, and conversely, the cost of computation is dominated by movement: under the roofline model¹¹, workloads whose arithmetic intensity falls below the hardware's ridge point are bandwidth-bound, and the dominant AI and scientific kernels sit deep in this regime². The leverage for improving sustainable computing therefore lies almost entirely in movement, not in operators (Fig. 1b).

Software-defined interconnect and its benefit-threshold
The structural defect of fixed-topology machines is that routing is frozen at fabrication. SDI dissolves this constraint by separating a control plane from a data plane within the chip, exactly as software-defined networking did for wide-area networks¹², but at nanosecond rather than millisecond granularity and at word rather than packet granularity. A programmable crossbar fabric can establish or break any port-to-port connection under the direction of a controller that maps each movement primitive to a set of switch states (Fig. 2a).

The immediate objection is that reconfiguration is not free: rewriting the fabric costs time and energy. We resolve this with a closed-form criterion. The critical engineering insight is that the latter is achievable with a double-buffered configuration register: while the current primitive computes, the next primitive's topology is preloaded into a shadow register and committed by an atomic swap the instant computation ends (Fig. 2b). We show that for a representative 64×64 crossbar the configuration store is ~4 kbit, writable in ~4 μs, so that for inference batches of millisecond scale the reconfiguration overhead falls below 0.5% of runtime, while the configuration energy (~4 pJ) is dwarfed by the saved memory-access energy (nanojoule to microjoule scale). The threshold is therefore satisfied by orders of magnitude across the dense-compute regime (Fig. 2c). This is the formal sense in which topology-centric computing is not merely attractive but provably advantageous: the four jointly sufficient conditions are large model or data scale, variable parallelism strategy, communication-dominated execution, and a requirement for cross-workload programmability — precisely the conditions of modern AI.

A reconfigurable fabric also unifies, rather than competes with, the major non-von-Neumann research directions. In-memory and near-memory computing shrink the operator-to-storage distance¹³; dataflow architectures stream intermediate state¹⁴; wafer-scale integration collapses inter-chip distance into on-wafer metal¹⁵. Each addresses how much is moved or how far; SDI addresses how it is routed, and composes orthogonally with all of them (Fig. 2d).

The α-barrier on emergent intelligent behaviour
If topology is a first-class resource, a natural question follows: does the richness of a system's reconfigurable network bound what it can do? We answer in the affirmative by analysing a complexity functional whose form is fixed by thermodynamic and device-physics constraints. A system's structural integration S_c and its dynamical richness T_c combine not additively but multiplicatively, and crucially their physical coupling Γ enters exponentially through a coefficient α set entirely by device physics — the logarithm of the number of distinguishable states a node can occupy (Fig. 3a):

The exponential placement of the coupling term is not a modelling choice but follows from treating structure–function alignment as an information gain near a phase transition¹⁶. Its consequence is stark. For a binary-digital substrate each node has two distinguishable states, fixing α = ln 2 ≈ 0.69; once training converges the structure–function coupling Γ freezes. Even at the optimal coupling Γ → 0.5, the achievable complexity is bounded above by CST_max ≈ 0.35 — permanently below the first emergence threshold we locate at 1/√2 ≈ 0.707 (Fig. 3b). We term this the α-barrier: no amount of parameter scaling within a fixed-topology, binary-digital architecture can cross it, because scaling adds nodes without raising α or unfreezing Γ.

The barrier is escaped only by raising α (multi-state devices) or by continuously re-coupling structure to function at runtime (Γ → optimum) — that is, precisely by the runtime topological reconfiguration that SDI provides (Fig. 3c). This closes the argument: the same mechanism that is provably energy-advantageous at the engineering layer is also the physically necessary lever at the intelligence layer. The convergence of recent industrial systems — wave-based expert scheduling, dataflow streaming, wafer-scale fabrics — can be read as an unforced migration toward this lever, whether or not it is named as such¹⁷⁻¹⁹.

Discussion
Topology-centric computing reframes the central design question of intelligent computing. It is no longer "how do we make each node faster?" but "how do we shape a dynamic hardware graph so that useful state moves as little, as locally, and under as little global synchronization as possible?" The two results we report bound this question from opposite ends: the benefit-threshold establishes when reconfiguration pays at the engineering layer, and the α-barrier establishes why reconfiguration is necessary at the intelligence layer.

Several boundary conditions delimit the claims. The benefit-threshold assumes communication-dominated, dense-compute workloads; it does not apply to control-dominated general-purpose computing, for which the fixed-topology von Neumann machine remains optimal. The α-barrier, and the complexity functional from which it follows, rests on a coupling term whose analytic derivation from symmetry-breaking is developed in a companion work and should at present be read as a falsifiable conjecture rather than a settled theorem: it would be refuted if a strictly feedforward, structurally frozen network demonstrated cross-domain autonomous adaptation without retraining. We regard this falsifiability as a feature, not a weakness, of a physical theory of computing.

The path forward, on this account, is not faster processors but smarter networks — interconnects that reshape their topology to match the dataflow of whatever is being computed, attacking the energy and the capability problem at the same physical locus.

Abstract
The von Neumann architecture has organized computation around processor-centric nodes for eight decades, treating the interconnect as a passive transport layer. This review argues that the assumption is now the binding constraint on sustainable intelligent computing. Across modern artificial-intelligence (AI) workloads, data movement consumes roughly 90% of energy while arithmetic consumes only about 10%, and this ratio has worsened for three decades as logic energy fell with transistor scaling while memory-access energy stagnated after the end of Dennard scaling. We synthesize evidence across general-purpose, AI, high-performance and signal-processing computing to show that the bottleneck is fundamentally topological: performance is increasingly a property of how nodes, memories, links and routes are related, not of the nodes themselves. On this basis we review and consolidate an emerging paradigm, topology-centric computing, in which the interconnect is promoted from substrate to a first-class, programmable computational resource. We identify software-defined interconnect (SDI) as the enabling mechanism, present a closed-form benefit-threshold establishing when runtime reconfiguration is provably worthwhile, and describe a double-buffered shadow-register scheme that hides reconfiguration latency at nanosecond granularity. We further review a complexity-bound argument — the α-barrier — linking a network's reconfigurable complexity to its capacity for emergent intelligent behaviour, and re-read recent industrial systems as an unforced migration toward this paradigm. We close with five open research questions. The path toward sustainable intelligent computing, we conclude, lies in smarter networks rather than faster processors.

Keywords: topology-centric computing; software-defined interconnect; data movement; sustainable intelligent computing; reconfigurable architecture; wafer-scale integration

1. Introduction
1.1 The structural crisis of an eighty-year paradigm
The von Neumann architecture has anchored computation on processor-centric nodes since 1945, an arrangement so successful that it remains the dominant template six decades later [1]. Its core philosophy treats the processor as protagonist, the memory as supporting actor and the interconnect as servant. Yet the assumption beneath it — that the interconnect is a transparent transport layer — has quietly become the binding constraint on sustainable computing.

The evidence is now unambiguous. Across modern AI workloads, the energy devoted to moving data dwarfs that devoted to arithmetic. A single off-chip memory access costs hundreds of times the energy of a floating-point multiply [2], and IBM Research reports that computation accounts for only about 10% of AI-workload energy while data movement consumes the other ~90% [3]. This ratio has widened for three decades: after Dennard scaling ended near 2006, logic energy continued to fall with voltage scaling, but dynamic memory, which relies on capacitor charge–discharge, did not benefit from the same trend [4]. The result is a thermodynamically unsustainable trajectory in which each model generation demands exponentially more energy for sub-linear gains [5,6].

1.2 Three walls converge into one
This crisis is best understood as the convergence of three long-recognized "walls." The memory wall, named by Wulf and McKee in 1995 [4], describes the widening processor–memory performance gap. The communication wall describes how, at cluster scale, collective-communication overhead grows faster than compute, with collective operations such as all-reduce and all-to-all consuming a substantial fraction of distributed-training step time [6,7]. The energy wall describes the hundreds-fold gap between an off-chip access and an arithmetic operation [2]. These occur at different levels — the processor–memory interface, the inter-node network and the whole-system energy flow — but they point to a single physical fact: in the von Neumann paradigm, it is data movement, not data processing, that governs efficiency, performance and scalability. We follow recent usage in unifying them as the data-movement wall.

1.3 Scope and contribution of this review
The prevailing response has been to strengthen the node, yet the more aggressively a node is strengthened, the more system performance is dictated by the relations among nodes [8,9]. This review consolidates an alternative whose center of gravity is the network rather than the node. Section 2 establishes the operator–movement decomposition that motivates the shift. Section 3 surveys data movement across four computing domains. Section 4 argues why operator optimization has reached diminishing returns. Section 5 reviews software-defined interconnect as the enabling mechanism and consolidates its benefit-threshold. Section 6 reviews the complexity-bound (α-barrier) argument linking topology to emergent behaviour. Section 7 re-reads industrial evidence. Section 8 offers an Outlook with five open questions. Unlike prior surveys that treat the interconnect as one engineering concern among many, this review elevates topology to the organizing principle of the field.

2. The operator–movement decomposition
2.1 A graph view of computation
Any computational task can be represented as a directed acyclic graph in which vertices are operators and edges are data dependencies [10]. Execution means delivering the right data, along the right path, to the right operator at the right time. This admits a weakly coupled decomposition into an operator space O(V) and a movement space M(E):

T=O(V)∘M(E)+Γ(O,M),
where the coupling term Γ is a higher-order effect, so that what is computed and how data are routed can be described and optimized independently.

2.2 The data-movement dominance regularity
Synthesizing measured energies from Horowitz (45 nm) [2] and the Eyeriss analysis (65 nm) [11], Table 1 shows that off-chip access exceeds a multiply–accumulate by ~200-fold. Defining the movement-energy ratio η = E_move/E_total, the literature converges on η ≥ 0.9 for AI, η ≈ 0.5–0.8 for high-performance and signal-processing workloads, and η > 0.5 across all data-intensive regimes. This is corroborated by the roofline model [12]: workloads below the hardware ridge point are bandwidth-bound, and dominant AI and scientific kernels sit deep in this regime. The leverage for sustainable computing therefore lies in movement, not operators.

3. Data movement across four computing domains
3.1 General-purpose computing
A CPU's multilevel cache hierarchy is itself a hierarchical movement network; each cache miss is a movement failure. Horowitz's data show the per-instruction control overhead (~70 pJ) far exceeds ALU arithmetic (0.1–3.7 pJ) [2]. Even in nominally compute-intensive general workloads, a large share of energy is spent moving data.

3.2 Intelligent computing
The core operator of AI is the multiply–accumulate; differentiation lies in dataflow, not operators. Eyeriss showed that row-stationary dataflow minimizes energy by maximizing reuse across register, array and buffer levels [11]. At system scale, wafer-scale fabrics that eliminate inter-chip movement, rather than faster arithmetic, deliver the decisive gains [13]; IBM NorthPole reports 47× higher speed and 73× higher energy efficiency than comparable GPUs by an extreme near-memory design [14]. In AI, the interconnect is performance.

3.3 High-performance computing
Stencil kernels exhibit arithmetic intensity of only 0.33–0.56 FLOP/byte [15], and the choice of interconnect topology — Dragonfly, fat-tree, torus — often influences real performance more than node peak FLOPS. In the domain that most needs performance, the network outweighs the node.

3.4 Signal processing
The fast Fourier transform's subtlety lies not in its butterfly operator but in the inter-stage data-reordering pattern; competing FFT hardware architectures differ chiefly in movement topology. Kung's 1978 systolic array was an early movement-optimization landmark [16]. Four decades of signal-processing architecture is, in effect, a history of movement optimization (Table 2).

4. Why operator optimization has reached diminishing returns
A natural objection is that mathematics contains infinitely many operations. This conflates two levels. Hardware operators converge to a small finite set: under IEEE 754, the irreducible atomic set is {ADD, MUL, AND, XOR, CMP, SHIFT} [17]. Transcendental functions reduce to shift-add via CORDIC [18]; exponentials reduce to multiply-add via polynomial approximation [19]. By Weierstrass approximation [20] and these reduction methods, any deterministic continuous operation in finite precision is approximable by a bounded composition of no more than ten atomic operators (Table 3). Because the operator space is so convergent, the determinant of efficiency can only lie in movement: the optimization direction for sustainable computing must shift from "faster operators" to "fewer movements."

5. Software-defined interconnect
5.1 From design-time fixation to runtime programmability
The structural defect of fixed-topology machines is that routing is frozen at fabrication; the CPU cache hierarchy, the GPU systolic dataflow and the ASIC network-on-chip are all set at tape-out [21]. SDI dissolves this by separating a control plane from a data plane within the chip, as software-defined networking did for wide-area networks [22], but at nanosecond rather than millisecond granularity and at word rather than packet granularity. A programmable crossbar establishes or breaks any port-to-port connection under a controller that maps each movement primitive to a set of switch states, formalized as R_runtime = C(G_task, H_state).

The four jointly sufficient conditions are large model/data scale, variable parallelism, communication-dominated execution and cross-workload programmability — precisely the conditions of modern AI.

5.3 Orthogonal composition with non-von-Neumann directions
SDI unifies rather than competes with the major research directions. In-memory and near-memory computing shrink the operator-to-storage distance [23]; dataflow architectures stream intermediate state [24]; wafer-scale integration collapses inter-chip distance into on-wafer metal [13]. Each addresses how much or how far; SDI addresses how it is routed, composing orthogonally with all of them.

6. The α-barrier: topology and emergent behaviour
If topology is a first-class resource, does the richness of a reconfigurable network bound what a system can do? An emerging complexity-bound argument answers in the affirmative. A system's structural integration S_c and dynamical richness T_c combine multiplicatively, and their physical coupling Γ enters exponentially through a coefficient α fixed by device physics — the logarithm of the number of distinguishable states per node:

For a binary-digital substrate, each node has two distinguishable states, fixing α = ln 2 ≈ 0.69; after training, the coupling Γ freezes. Even at optimal coupling, the achievable complexity is bounded above by ≈ 0.35, below a first emergence threshold near 1/√2 ≈ 0.707. This α-barrier implies that parameter scaling within a fixed-topology, binary-digital architecture cannot, by itself, cross the threshold, because scaling adds nodes without raising α or unfreezing Γ. The barrier is escaped only by raising α (multi-state devices) or by continuously re-coupling structure to function at runtime — that is, by the runtime topological reconfiguration SDI provides. This argument should at present be read as a falsifiable conjecture whose analytic derivation is developed elsewhere, rather than a settled theorem; we return to its falsifiability in Section 8.

7. Industrial evidence of an unforced migration
A striking feature of the present moment is that the industry is moving toward topology-centric design under pressure, whether or not it uses the term. DeepSeek-V3 achieved near-full computation–communication overlap through dual-pipeline scheduling and restricted routing, reducing effective all-to-all overhead [25]. Dataflow accelerators such as the SambaNova RDU explicitly frame inference as a data-movement problem, keeping intermediate state local through coarse-grained streaming [24]. Cerebras WSE-3 places 900,000 cores, 44 GB on-chip SRAM and 125 PFLOPS on a single wafer [13], elevating the fabric itself into the primary design resource. These systems do not converge on one implementation; they converge on one principle — reduce movement, exploit locality, internalize routing, and let graph structure participate in performance.

8. Outlook
Five questions delimit the agenda for topology-centric computing. First, the minimum viable granularity of SDI: at PE, core or chip level, how does the benefit-threshold scale with granularity? Second, the compilation challenge: can arbitrary computation graphs be automatically compiled into optimal operator–movement–reconfiguration sequences, and can existing MLIR/TVM frameworks be extended directly? Third, the falsifiability of the α-barrier: what experimental evidence would elevate it from conjecture to theorem, and conversely, would a strictly feedforward, structurally frozen network demonstrating cross-domain adaptation without retraining refute it? Fourth, the substrate physics: which combination of multi-state devices, photonic transport and reconfigurable interposers most efficiently raises α and unfreezes Γ? Fifth, the boundary of applicability: topology-centric computing is advantageous only for communication-dominated, dense-compute workloads and does not displace the fixed-topology von Neumann machine for control-dominated general-purpose computing.

The path toward sustainable intelligent computing, this review concludes, runs not through faster processors but through smarter networks — interconnects that reshape their topology to match the dataflow of whatever is being computed, attacking the energy problem and the capability problem at the same physical locus.

Abstract
The von Neumann architecture has organized computation around processor-centric nodes for eight decades, treating the interconnect as a passive transport layer. This review argues that the assumption is now the binding constraint on sustainable intelligent computing. Across modern artificial-intelligence (AI) workloads, data movement consumes roughly 90% of energy while arithmetic consumes only about 10%, and this ratio has worsened for three decades as logic energy fell with transistor scaling while memory-access energy stagnated after the end of Dennard scaling. We synthesize evidence across general-purpose, AI, high-performance and signal-processing computing to show that the bottleneck is fundamentally topological: performance is increasingly a property of how nodes, memories, links and routes are related, not of the nodes themselves. On this basis we review and consolidate an emerging paradigm, topology-centric computing, in which the interconnect is promoted from substrate to a first-class, programmable computational resource. We identify software-defined interconnect (SDI) as the enabling mechanism, consolidate a closed-form benefit-threshold establishing when runtime reconfiguration is provably worthwhile, and describe a double-buffered shadow-register scheme that hides reconfiguration latency at nanosecond granularity. We show that SDI composes orthogonally with in-memory, near-memory, dataflow and wafer-scale approaches, and we re-read recent industrial systems as an unforced migration toward this paradigm. We close with five open research questions. The path toward sustainable intelligent computing, we conclude, lies in smarter networks rather than faster processors.

Keywords: topology-centric computing; software-defined interconnect; data movement; sustainable intelligent computing; reconfigurable architecture; wafer-scale integration

1. Introduction
1.1 The structural crisis of an eighty-year paradigm
The von Neumann architecture has anchored computation on processor-centric nodes since 1945, an arrangement so successful that it remains the dominant template six decades later [1]. Its core philosophy treats the processor as protagonist, the memory as supporting actor and the interconnect as servant. Yet the assumption beneath it — that the interconnect is a transparent transport layer — has quietly become the binding constraint on sustainable computing.

The evidence is now unambiguous. Across modern AI workloads, the energy devoted to moving data dwarfs that devoted to arithmetic. A single off-chip memory access costs hundreds of times the energy of a floating-point multiply [2], and IBM Research reports that computation accounts for only about 10% of AI-workload energy while data movement consumes the other ~90% [3]. This ratio has widened for three decades: after Dennard scaling ended near 2006, logic energy continued to fall with voltage scaling, but dynamic memory, which relies on capacitor charge–discharge, did not benefit from the same trend [4]. The result is a thermodynamically unsustainable trajectory in which each model generation demands exponentially more energy for sub-linear gains [5,6].

1.2 Three walls converge into one
This crisis is best understood as the convergence of three long-recognized "walls." The memory wall, named by Wulf and McKee in 1995 [4], describes the widening processor–memory performance gap. The communication wall describes how, at cluster scale, collective-communication overhead grows faster than compute, with collective operations such as all-reduce and all-to-all consuming a substantial fraction of distributed-training step time [6,7]. The energy wall describes the hundreds-fold gap between an off-chip access and an arithmetic operation [2]. These occur at different levels — the processor–memory interface, the inter-node network and the whole-system energy flow — but they point to a single physical fact: in the von Neumann paradigm, it is data movement, not data processing, that governs efficiency, performance and scalability. We follow recent usage in unifying them as the data-movement wall.

1.3 Scope and contribution of this review
The prevailing response has been to strengthen the node, yet the more aggressively a node is strengthened, the more system performance is dictated by the relations among nodes [8,9]. This review consolidates an alternative whose center of gravity is the network rather than the node. Section 2 establishes the operator–movement decomposition that motivates the shift. Section 3 surveys data movement across four computing domains. Section 4 argues why operator optimization has reached diminishing returns. Section 5 reviews software-defined interconnect as the enabling mechanism and consolidates its benefit-threshold. Section 6 re-reads industrial evidence as a migration toward the paradigm. Section 7 offers an Outlook with five open questions. Unlike prior surveys that treat the interconnect as one engineering concern among many, this review elevates topology to the organizing principle of the field.

2. The operator–movement decomposition
2.1 A graph view of computation
Any computational task can be represented as a directed acyclic graph in which vertices are operators and edges are data dependencies [10]. Execution means delivering the right data, along the right path, to the right operator at the right time. This admits a weakly coupled decomposition into an operator space O(V) and a movement space M(E):

T=O(V)∘M(E)+Γ(O,M),
where the coupling term Γ is a higher-order effect, so that what is computed and how data are routed can be described and optimized independently.

2.2 The data-movement dominance regularity
Synthesizing measured energies from Horowitz (45 nm) [2] and the Eyeriss analysis (65 nm) [11], Table 1 shows that off-chip access exceeds a multiply–accumulate by ~200-fold. Defining the movement-energy ratio η = E_move/E_total, the literature converges on η ≥ 0.9 for AI, η ≈ 0.5–0.8 for high-performance and signal-processing workloads, and η > 0.5 across all data-intensive regimes. This is corroborated by the roofline model [12]: workloads below the hardware ridge point are bandwidth-bound, and dominant AI and scientific kernels sit deep in this regime. The leverage for sustainable computing therefore lies in movement, not operators.

3. Data movement across four computing domains
3.1 General-purpose computing
A CPU's multilevel cache hierarchy is itself a hierarchical movement network; each cache miss is a movement failure. Horowitz's data show the per-instruction control overhead (~70 pJ) far exceeds ALU arithmetic (0.1–3.7 pJ) [2]. Even in nominally compute-intensive general workloads, a large share of energy is spent moving data.

3.2 Intelligent computing
The core operator of AI is the multiply–accumulate; differentiation lies in dataflow, not operators. Eyeriss showed that row-stationary dataflow minimizes energy by maximizing reuse across register, array and buffer levels [11]. At system scale, wafer-scale fabrics that eliminate inter-chip movement, rather than faster arithmetic, deliver the decisive gains [13]; IBM NorthPole reports 47× higher speed and 73× higher energy efficiency than comparable GPUs through an extreme near-memory design [14]. In AI, the interconnect is performance.

3.3 High-performance computing
Stencil kernels exhibit arithmetic intensity of only 0.33–0.56 FLOP/byte [15], and the choice of interconnect topology — Dragonfly, fat-tree, torus — often influences real performance more than node peak FLOPS. In the domain that most needs performance, the network outweighs the node.

3.4 Signal processing
The fast Fourier transform's subtlety lies not in its butterfly operator but in the inter-stage data-reordering pattern; competing FFT hardware architectures differ chiefly in movement topology. Kung's 1978 systolic array was an early movement-optimization landmark [16]. Four decades of signal-processing architecture is, in effect, a history of movement optimization (Table 2).

4. Why operator optimization has reached diminishing returns
A natural objection is that mathematics contains infinitely many operations. This conflates two levels. Hardware operators converge to a small finite set: under IEEE 754, the irreducible atomic set is {ADD, MUL, AND, XOR, CMP, SHIFT} [17]. Transcendental functions reduce to shift-add via CORDIC [18]; exponentials reduce to multiply-add via polynomial approximation [19]. By Weierstrass approximation [20] and these reduction methods, any deterministic continuous operation in finite precision is approximable by a bounded composition of no more than ten atomic operators (Table 3). Because the operator space is so convergent, the determinant of efficiency can only lie in movement: the optimization direction for sustainable computing must shift from "faster operators" to "fewer movements."

5. Software-defined interconnect
5.1 From design-time fixation to runtime programmability
The structural defect of fixed-topology machines is that routing is frozen at fabrication; the CPU cache hierarchy, the GPU systolic dataflow and the ASIC network-on-chip are all set at tape-out [21]. SDI dissolves this by separating a control plane from a data plane within the chip, as software-defined networking did for wide-area networks [22], but at nanosecond rather than millisecond granularity and at word rather than packet granularity. A programmable crossbar establishes or breaks any port-to-port connection under a controller that maps each movement primitive to a set of switch states, formalized as R_runtime = C(G_task, H_state).

The latter is achievable with a double-buffered configuration register: while the current primitive computes, the next topology is preloaded into a shadow register and committed by an atomic swap when computation ends. For a representative 64×64 crossbar the configuration store is ~4 kbit, writable in ~4 μs, so for millisecond-scale inference batches the overhead falls below 0.5% of runtime, while the ~4 pJ configuration energy is dwarfed by saved memory-access energy (nanojoule–microjoule scale). The four jointly sufficient conditions are large model/data scale, variable parallelism, communication-dominated execution and cross-workload programmability — precisely the conditions of modern AI.

5.3 Orthogonal composition with non-von-Neumann directions
SDI unifies rather than competes with the major research directions. In-memory and near-memory computing shrink the operator-to-storage distance [23]; dataflow architectures stream intermediate state [24]; wafer-scale integration collapses inter-chip distance into on-wafer metal [13]. Each addresses how much is moved or how far; SDI addresses how it is routed, composing orthogonally with all of them.

6. Industrial evidence of an unforced migration
A striking feature of the present moment is that the industry is moving toward topology-centric design under pressure, whether or not it uses the term. DeepSeek-V3 achieved near-full computation–communication overlap through dual-pipeline scheduling and restricted routing, reducing effective all-to-all overhead [25]. Dataflow accelerators such as the SambaNova RDU explicitly frame inference as a data-movement problem, keeping intermediate state local through coarse-grained streaming [24]. Cerebras WSE-3 places 900,000 cores, 44 GB on-chip SRAM and 125 PFLOPS on a single wafer [13], elevating the fabric itself into the primary design resource. These systems do not converge on one implementation; they converge on one principle — reduce movement, exploit locality, internalize routing, and let graph structure participate in performance.

7. Outlook
Five questions delimit the agenda for topology-centric computing. First, the minimum viable granularity of SDI: at PE, core or chip level, how does the benefit-threshold scale with granularity? Second, the compilation challenge: can arbitrary computation graphs be automatically compiled into optimal operator–movement–reconfiguration sequences, and can existing MLIR/TVM frameworks be extended directly? Third, the physical substrate: which combination of multi-state devices, photonic transport and reconfigurable interposers most efficiently reduces reconfiguration cost and raises usable bandwidth? Fourth, the verification gap: how can the benefit-threshold be validated end-to-end on real hardware, and what fraction of theoretical movement savings survives contention, congestion and synchronization overhead? Fifth, the boundary of applicability: topology-centric computing is advantageous only for communication-dominated, dense-compute workloads and does not displace the fixed-topology von Neumann machine for control-dominated general-purpose computing.

The path toward sustainable intelligent computing, this review concludes, runs not through faster processors but through smarter networks — interconnects that reshape their topology to match the dataflow of whatever is being computed, attacking the energy problem at its physical locus.

Abstract
The von Neumann architecture has organized computation around processor-centric nodes for eight decades, treating the interconnect as a passive transport layer. This review argues that the assumption is now the binding constraint on sustainable intelligent computing. Across modern artificial-intelligence (AI) workloads, data movement consumes roughly 90% of energy while arithmetic consumes only about 10%, and this ratio has worsened for three decades as logic energy fell with transistor scaling while memory-access energy stagnated after the end of Dennard scaling. We synthesize evidence across general-purpose, AI, high-performance and signal-processing computing to show that the bottleneck is fundamentally topological: performance is increasingly a property of how nodes, memories, links and routes are related, not of the nodes themselves. On this basis we consolidate an emerging paradigm, topology-centric computing, in which the interconnect is promoted from substrate to a first-class, programmable computational resource. We review software-defined interconnect (SDI) as the enabling mechanism, clarify the qualitative conditions under which runtime reconfiguration yields a net benefit, and survey how SDI composes orthogonally with in-memory, near-memory, dataflow and wafer-scale approaches. We further re-read recent industrial systems as an unforced migration toward this paradigm, and close with five open research questions. The path toward sustainable intelligent computing, we conclude, lies in smarter networks rather than faster processors.

Keywords: topology-centric computing; software-defined interconnect; data movement; sustainable intelligent computing; reconfigurable architecture; wafer-scale integration

1. Introduction
1.1 The structural crisis of an eighty-year paradigm
The von Neumann architecture has anchored computation on processor-centric nodes since 1945, an arrangement so successful that it remains the dominant template six decades later [1]. As von Neumann's original design envisioned, the processor is the protagonist, the memory a supporting actor and the interconnect a servant. Yet the assumption beneath it—that the interconnect is a transparent transport layer—has quietly become the binding constraint on sustainable computing.

The evidence is now unambiguous. Across modern AI workloads, the energy devoted to moving data dwarfs that devoted to arithmetic. As Horowitz demonstrated in his influential 2014 analysis, a single off-chip memory access costs hundreds of times the energy of a floating-point multiply [2]; IBM Research reports that computation accounts for only about 10% of AI-workload energy while data movement consumes the other ~90% [3]. This ratio has widened for three decades: after Dennard scaling ended near 2006, logic energy continued to fall with voltage scaling, but dynamic memory, which relies on capacitor charge–discharge, did not benefit from the same trend [4]. The result is a thermodynamically unsustainable trajectory in which each model generation demands exponentially more energy for sub-linear gains [5,6].

1.2 Three walls converge into one
This crisis is best understood as the convergence of three long-recognized "walls." The memory wall, named by Wulf and McKee in 1995 [4], describes the widening processor–memory performance gap. The communication wall describes how, at cluster scale, collective-communication overhead grows faster than compute, with collective operations such as all-reduce and all-to-all consuming a substantial fraction of distributed-training step time [6,7]. The energy wall describes the hundreds-fold gap between an off-chip access and an arithmetic operation [2]. These occur at different levels—the processor–memory interface, the inter-node network and the whole-system energy flow—but they point to a single physical fact: in the von Neumann paradigm, it is data movement, not data processing, that governs efficiency, performance and scalability. We follow recent usage in unifying them as the data-movement wall.

1.3 Scope and structure of this review
The prevailing response has been to strengthen the node, yet the more aggressively a node is strengthened, the more system performance is dictated by the relations among nodes [8,9]. This review consolidates an alternative whose center of gravity is the network rather than the node. Section 2 establishes the operator–movement decomposition that motivates the shift. Section 3 surveys data movement across four computing domains. Section 4 argues why operator optimization has reached diminishing returns. Section 5 reviews software-defined interconnect as the enabling mechanism. Section 6 re-reads industrial evidence as a migration toward the paradigm. Section 7 offers an Outlook with five open questions. Unlike prior surveys that treat the interconnect as one engineering concern among many, this review elevates topology to the organizing principle of the field.

2. The operator–movement decomposition
2.1 A graph view of computation
Any computational task can be represented as a directed acyclic graph in which vertices are operators and edges are data dependencies [10]. Execution means delivering the right data, along the right path, to the right operator at the right time. This admits a weakly coupled decomposition into an operator space O(V) and a movement space M(E):

T=O(V)∘M(E)+Γ(O,M),
where the coupling term Γ is a higher-order effect, so that what is computed and how data are routed can be described and optimized independently.

2.2 The data-movement dominance regularity
Synthesizing measured energies from Horowitz (45 nm) [2] and the Eyeriss analysis (65 nm) [11], Table 1 shows that off-chip access exceeds a multiply–accumulate by approximately 200-fold. Defining the movement-energy ratio η = E_move/E_total, the literature converges on η ≥ 0.9 for AI, η ≈ 0.5–0.8 for high-performance and signal-processing workloads, and η > 0.5 across all data-intensive regimes. This is corroborated by the roofline model [12]: workloads below the hardware ridge point are bandwidth-bound, and the dominant AI and scientific kernels sit deep in this regime. The leverage for sustainable computing therefore lies in movement, not operators.

3. Data movement across four computing domains
3.1 General-purpose computing
A CPU's multilevel cache hierarchy is itself a hierarchical movement network; each cache miss is a movement failure. Horowitz's data show the per-instruction control overhead (~70 pJ) far exceeds ALU arithmetic (0.1–3.7 pJ) [2]. Even in nominally compute-intensive general workloads, a large share of energy is spent moving data.

3.2 Intelligent computing
The core operator of AI is the multiply–accumulate; differentiation lies in dataflow, not operators. Eyeriss showed that row-stationary dataflow minimizes energy by maximizing reuse across register, array and buffer levels [11]. At system scale, wafer-scale fabrics that eliminate inter-chip movement, rather than faster arithmetic, deliver the decisive gains [13]; IBM NorthPole reports 47× higher speed and 73× higher energy efficiency than comparable GPUs through an extreme near-memory design [14]. In AI, the interconnect is performance.

3.3 High-performance computing
Stencil kernels exhibit arithmetic intensity of only 0.33–0.56 FLOP/byte [15], and the choice of interconnect topology—Dragonfly, fat-tree, torus—often influences real performance more than node peak FLOPS. In the domain that most needs performance, the network outweighs the node.

3.4 Signal processing
The fast Fourier transform's subtlety lies not in its butterfly operator but in the inter-stage data-reordering pattern; competing FFT hardware architectures differ chiefly in movement topology. Kung's 1978 systolic array was an early movement-optimization landmark, performing many computations per memory access without increasing I/O bandwidth [16]. Four decades of signal-processing architecture is, in effect, a history of movement optimization (Table 2).

4. Why operator optimization has reached diminishing returns
A natural objection is that mathematics contains infinitely many operations. This conflates two levels. Hardware operators converge to a small finite set: under IEEE 754 [17], the irreducible atomic set is {ADD, MUL, AND, XOR, CMP, SHIFT}. Transcendental functions reduce to shift-add via CORDIC [18]; exponentials reduce to multiply-add via polynomial approximation [19]. By the Weierstrass approximation theorem [20] together with these reduction methods, any deterministic continuous operation in finite precision is approximable by a bounded composition of no more than ten atomic operators (Table 3). Because the operator space is so convergent, the determinant of efficiency can only lie in movement; the optimization direction for sustainable computing must shift from "faster operators" to "fewer movements."

5. Software-defined interconnect
5.1 From design-time fixation to runtime programmability
The structural defect of fixed-topology machines is that routing is frozen at fabrication; the CPU cache hierarchy, the GPU systolic dataflow and the ASIC network-on-chip are all set at tape-out. The MAERI architecture demonstrated the value of making the interconnect within an accelerator reconfigurable [21], an early on-chip expression of this idea. More broadly, software-defined interconnect dissolves the constraint by separating a control plane from a data plane within the chip, as software-defined networking did for wide-area networks [22], but at finer temporal and spatial granularity. Conceptually, a programmable fabric establishes or breaks connections under a controller that maps each movement primitive to a configuration of the fabric, a relation we denote R_runtime = C(G_task, H_state). In this view the interconnect ceases to be passive infrastructure and becomes an active optimization dimension.

5.2 When runtime reconfiguration pays
Reconfiguration is not free: rewriting the fabric incurs a configuration cost in both time and energy. The central qualitative principle, recurring across the reconfigurable-computing literature, is that runtime reconfiguration yields a net benefit only when the movement energy and time it saves exceed the configuration overhead it incurs. Two consequences follow. First, the energy condition is easily satisfied in data-intensive regimes, because the saved memory-access energy is large relative to the cost of writing a compact fabric configuration. Second, the time condition need not appear on the critical path: in principle the configuration of the next operation can be overlapped with the execution of the current one, so that switching cost is hidden rather than added [21]. Consolidating these observations, runtime reconfiguration is most advantageous under four jointly sufficient conditions—large model or data scale, variable parallelism strategy, communication-dominated execution and a requirement for cross-workload programmability—which are precisely the conditions of modern AI. The precise quantitative form of this benefit condition, and its hardware realization, are the subject of ongoing engineering work and are deliberately left outside the scope of this review.

5.3 Orthogonal composition with non-von-Neumann directions
SDI unifies rather than competes with the major research directions. In-memory and near-memory computing shrink the operator-to-storage distance [23]; dataflow architectures stream intermediate state, keeping it local across what were once kernel boundaries [24]; wafer-scale integration collapses inter-chip distance into on-wafer metal [13]. Each addresses how much is moved or how far; a reconfigurable interconnect addresses how it is routed, and composes orthogonally with all of them. This complementarity suggests that the future of intelligent computing is not a single winning device but a coordinated stack in which a programmable fabric organizes heterogeneous compute and memory resources.

6. Industrial evidence of an unforced migration
A striking feature of the present moment is that industry is moving toward topology-centric design under pressure, whether or not it uses the term. DeepSeek-V3 achieved near-full computation–communication overlap through dual-pipeline scheduling and restricted routing, reducing effective all-to-all overhead [25]. Dataflow accelerators such as the SambaNova RDU explicitly frame inference as a data-movement problem, keeping intermediate state local through coarse-grained streaming [24]. Cerebras WSE-3 places 900,000 cores, 44 GB of on-chip SRAM and 125 PFLOPS on a single wafer [13], elevating the fabric itself into the primary design resource. These systems do not converge on one implementation; they converge on one principle—reduce movement, exploit locality, internalize routing, and let graph structure participate in performance. This convergence, arrived at independently by software-system and hardware teams, is perhaps the strongest evidence that the migration toward topology-centric computing is structural rather than stylistic.

7. Outlook
Five questions delimit the agenda for topology-centric computing. First, the minimum viable granularity of reconfiguration: at processing-element, core or chip level, how does the net benefit scale with granularity? Second, the compilation challenge: can arbitrary computation graphs be automatically compiled into efficient operator–movement sequences, and can existing compiler frameworks such as MLIR and TVM be extended directly? Third, the physical substrate: which combination of multi-state devices, photonic transport and reconfigurable interposers most efficiently lowers reconfiguration cost and raises usable bandwidth? Fourth, the verification gap: how can the benefit of reconfiguration be validated end-to-end on real hardware, and what fraction of the theoretical movement savings survives contention, congestion and synchronization overhead? Fifth, the boundary of applicability: topology-centric computing is advantageous only for communication-dominated, dense-compute workloads and does not displace the fixed-topology von Neumann machine for control-dominated general-purpose computing, which—as IBM researchers have noted—is likely to remain a hybrid of von Neumann and non-von-Neumann processors [3].

The path toward sustainable intelligent computing, this review concludes, runs not through faster processors but through smarter networks—interconnects that reshape their topology to match the dataflow of whatever is being computed, attacking the energy problem at its physical locus.
